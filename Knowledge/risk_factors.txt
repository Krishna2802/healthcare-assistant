Several factors increase the risk of developing type 2 diabetes. These factors interact with genetics, environment, and lifestyle choices.

1. Obesity and Overweight: Excess body fat, especially abdominal fat, increases insulin resistance, making it harder for the body to regulate blood sugar.

2. Physical Inactivity: Sedentary lifestyles contribute to weight gain and insulin resistance. Regular physical activity improves glucose uptake by muscles.

3. Unhealthy Diet: Diets high in processed foods, sugary beverages, refined grains, and saturated fats increase diabetes risk. A diet rich in fiber, whole grains, lean protein, and healthy fats reduces risk.

4. Age: The risk of type 2 diabetes increases with age, especially after 45 years, though it is increasingly seen in younger populations due to obesity.

5. Family History: A parent or sibling with type 2 diabetes significantly increases an individual's risk.

6. Hypertension and High Cholesterol: These metabolic conditions often cluster with diabetes in the metabolic syndrome, increasing overall risk.

7. Smoking and Alcohol: Smoking increases insulin resistance and contributes to cardiovascular damage. Excessive alcohol intake can impair glucose metabolism.

8. Gestational Diabetes: Women with gestational diabetes during pregnancy have a higher risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life.

9. Ethnicity: Certain ethnic groups, such as African Americans, Hispanic/Latino Americans, Native Americans, and South Asians, have a higher predisposition to type 2 diabetes.

Understanding these risk factors helps in prevention and early intervention.
